Team Practice & Competition Philosophy
18 principles every Knight golfer lives by
  • 1
    Playing Well does not equal Scoring Well. Knowing how to attack a course, playing smart, using low-risk / high-reward shots lets you score well even when ball-striking feels off.
  • 2
    Keep tee shots in play. Swing confidently with clubs that keep you away from OB, water, hazards, and bunkers.
  • 3
    Fewer double bogeys, not more birdies. Limiting doubles or worse will have you shooting low. Birdies are nice but not the path to team scores.
  • 4
    Accept mistakes. You will blade a chip, pick one clean from the sand. Accept it during the round. Dwell on it during practice when the round is over.
  • 5
    This game is hard. Persevere. Focus on habits, not outcomes. Build a routine and thought process for every shot. Remove second-guessing, especially after a bad hole.
  • 6
    Keep the ball on the ground near the green. A bump and run is safer than a lofted chip. You will rarely blade or chunk it over the green, and the ball can be read all the way in.
  • 7
    Speed Control beats Aim on putts. Missing left or right keeps you in position to make the next putt if your speed was right.
  • 8
    Embrace analytics. Understanding the most important numbers helps you focus practice on what matters most for your score.
  • 9
    Club fitting matters. Equipment matched to your swing maximizes the sweet spot and minimizes misses.
  • 10
    Learn to control the clubface. Draws, fades, high and low trajectories give you mid-round fixes and real scoring confidence.
  • 11
    Strength and flexibility. Strength train at least twice a week during the season. Flexibility exercises every day. Length and injury prevention both depend on it.
  • 12
    Keep it simple. One swing thought per shot. Fix the issue earliest in your sequence: Grip to Stance to Posture to Takeaway to Top to Downswing to Path to Delivery to Contact.
  • 13
    Practice randomly. After warm-up, hit different clubs every shot. Move putts and chips to different distances and lies. Practice how you play. Never hit driver back to back unless you went OB.
  • 14
    Understand wedge flights. Pros rarely hit full wedges. They flight them lower, compressed, with more controlled spin. Learning this will quickly lower your scores.
  • 15
    Know your carry yardages. Consistently hitting your 7-iron 170 once does not make 170 your yardage. Know your real, repeatable numbers.
  • 16
    Take your medicine. In trees, long grass or worse, do not go for the hero shot. Professional golfers make bogey 80% of the time from trouble because they accept it and do not snowball.
  • 17
    Study the course. Use 18Birdies and other apps to tour courses virtually. Map trouble, identify landing zones, decide your club off every tee before you arrive. Have a plan for every meet.
  • 18
    Have Fun! The most important point of all. Find ways to celebrate small milestones. The journey of getting better at this game is long. Enjoy every step of it.

Team Course Management Strategy
Varsity and JV, step by step, non-negotiable
Before the Round
  • 1
    View the course scouting report provided by your coach, or the scorecard if no report is available yet.
  • 2
    Circle the 6 highest handicap holes (1 = toughest). These are your bogey holes where you MUST sacrifice distance for accuracy.
  • 3
    Square the 3 lowest handicap holes. These are your par opportunities if you play smart off the tee and in the scoring zone.
On the Tee Box and Outside the Scoring Zone (more than 50 yards from center of green)
  • 1
    Identify areas to avoid: water, trees, sand. Determine exact distances to each.
  • 2
    Pick a landing target and determine the distance to that spot, NOT the pin.
  • 3
    Choose the club that guarantees landing 10-20 yards short of any trouble. Front edge of trap at 120 yards, back at 130 yards, hit your 150-yard club.
  • 4
    Aim for your shot shape. If you fade and water is left, aim 10 yards right of the water. Know your dispersion pattern for every club.
  • 5
    One swing thought after your pre-shot routine. ONE positive thought: Bump to lead side, Smooth tempo, Hips start downswing.
  • 6
    Commit to the shot. You have already done the thinking. Trust your skills. Swing with confidence.
Inside the Scoring Zone (inside 50 yards)
  • 1
    Aim for the safest part of the green and hit a club that guarantees you get there. Resist the temptation to aim at the pin. You cannot two-putt for bogey if you are not on the green.
  • 2
    First putt outside 10 feet is a lag putt. Tempo is everything. Get the ball close. Speed control is more important than the correct line. This is where double bogeys are saved.

Air Squats — Full Depth
10 reps

How to perform: Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes turned out slightly. Drive hips back and down, keeping chest tall and knees tracking over toes. Descend until thighs are parallel to the ground or lower — full depth. Drive through the heels to stand, squeezing glutes at the top.

⛳ Golf benefit: Loads glutes and quads, opens hips, and primes the lower body for the rotational power you need off the tee.

Banded Pull-Aparts

How to perform: Hold a light resistance band at shoulder height with both hands, arms extended straight in front of you. Keeping arms straight, pull the band apart by squeezing your shoulder blades together until your arms are fully extended to your sides. Control the return. Palms can face up or down — both work.

⛳ Golf benefit: Activates the rear deltoids and rotator cuff, stabilizing the shoulder joint through the full swing arc and preventing common golf shoulder injuries.

Twisting Lunges with a Club
5 reps each leg

How to perform: Hold a golf club horizontally across your chest or extended in front of you. Step forward into a deep lunge, lowering your back knee toward the ground. At the bottom of the lunge, rotate your torso fully toward the front leg — club follows the rotation. Pause, rotate back to center, then push off to standing. Complete all reps on one leg before switching.

⛳ Golf benefit: Combines hip flexor lengthening with thoracic rotation — exactly the sequencing your body needs for a full backswing and follow-through.

World's Greatest Stretch
5 reps each side

How to perform: Start in a push-up position. Step your right foot forward to the outside of your right hand. Drop your left knee gently to the ground. Reach your right hand up toward the ceiling, rotating your torso fully and following it with your eyes. Hold 1–2 seconds, return hand to the ground, then push your hips back into a hamstring stretch. Return to start and repeat on the left side.

⛳ Golf benefit: Opens the hip flexors, thoracic spine, and shoulder in one sequence — arguably the single most effective pre-round stretch for golfers.

Hip Gate Openers & Closers
10 each direction per side

How to perform: Stand on one leg (use a club for balance if needed). Lift the opposite knee to hip height. Openers: rotate the lifted leg outward like opening a gate, then lower. Closers: lift the knee and rotate it inward across the body, then lower. Keep your standing knee slightly bent and your hips level throughout. Complete all reps on one side before switching.

⛳ Golf benefit: Lubricates the hip joint and increases the range of external/internal hip rotation — directly improving your ability to rotate through the ball without restriction.

Leg Swings — Front/Back & Side to Side
10 reps each direction per leg

How to perform: Stand sideways to a wall or use a club for balance. Front/Back: swing one leg forward and backward in a relaxed, pendulum motion, letting momentum build gradually. Side to Side: face the wall, swing the same leg across your body (right to left) and out to the side, again using controlled momentum. Keep your core engaged and avoid letting your hips rotate. Complete all reps on one leg, then switch.

⛳ Golf benefit: Dynamically warms the hip flexors, hamstrings, and adductors — the muscles most responsible for your weight transfer and follow-through position.

90/90 Hip Rotations
1 minute

How to perform: Sit on the ground with both legs bent at 90°: front leg angled in front of you, back leg angled behind. Both knees form right angles. Keeping your torso tall, slowly rotate your hips to bring the front leg behind and the back leg forward, transitioning through center. Alternate sides smoothly for the full minute. Use your hands lightly on the ground for support if needed.

⛳ Golf benefit: Releases the hip flexors and capsule in both internal and external rotation — the two hip movements that drive power and separation in the golf swing.

Cat-Cow Stretches
10 reps

How to perform: Start on your hands and knees with wrists under shoulders and knees under hips. Cow: inhale, drop your belly toward the floor, lift your head and tailbone up, arching your back. Cat: exhale, round your spine toward the ceiling, tucking chin to chest and tailbone under. Move slowly between positions, letting your breath guide the pace.

⛳ Golf benefit: Mobilizes the entire spine — from the lumbar through the thoracic — reducing the stiffness and tightness that leads to back injuries during the golf swing.

⛳ Pre-Shot Routine — Putting Routine First ▼

Every putt outside 3 feet follows this exact sequence — no exceptions.

  1. Mark your ball. Use a flat marker. Always mark before lifting.
  2. Read the green from all angles. Walk to the low side of the putt. Crouch behind the ball and behind the hole. Look for the fall line — where would water drain? The second half of the putt breaks more than the first as the ball slows.
  3. Align your ball line. Draw a line on your ball. Aim it at your entry point — not the hole center, but where you want the ball to enter the cup given the break.
  4. Take your practice stroke from behind the ball. One or two smooth strokes rehearsing the tempo and length you need. Feel the distance.
  5. Step in and set the putter face. Align your putter face to your ball line. Set your feet parallel to it.
  6. One look. Look at the hole once. Look back at the ball. Do not look up again.
  7. Commit and stroke. The decision is made. Trust what you read. One smooth stroke with the tempo you rehearsed.
Lag putts (20+ feet): Walk to the halfway point — this is the best angle to feel the distance. Focus your practice stroke on the length of the putt, not the line. Speed control matters more than aim on any putt over 15 feet.

🏌️ Pre-Shot Routine — Chipping Routine First ▼

Use this routine for every chip — off the green, fringe, and bump-and-run shots.

  1. Select your landing spot first. Before choosing a club, pick the exact spot you want the ball to land on the green. A small patch of short grass, a shadow, a discoloration — something specific, not a general area.
  2. Apply the Rule of 12. Walk off steps to the landing spot and steps to the hole. Divide total steps by steps to landing spot. Subtract from 12. That is your club number.
  3. Set up correctly. Weight 60–65% on your front foot. Ball center to slightly back. Hands ahead of the ball with a slight shaft lean. Grip firm enough that the club does not flip.
  4. Two practice swings — brush the turf. Make two swings at the same tempo you will use, brushing the grass where the ball sits. Listen for a single clean contact sound, not a slap.
  5. Pick your target line. Set the clubface toward your landing spot, not the hole.
  6. One look at the landing spot. Eyes back to the ball. Commit.
  7. Swing with your body, not your hands. Pendulum motion. No scooping at impact.
Key thought: "Land it on the spot, let the green do the rest." Your only job is getting the ball to the landing spot at the right speed. The hole takes care of itself.

Rule of 12 — Club Selection MethodTechnique▼
  1. From your ball, take large steps to your landing spot (3 yards onto the green past the fringe). Example: 3 steps.
  2. Continue counting steps to the hole. Example: 12 steps total.
  3. Divide total steps by steps to landing spot: 12 ÷ 3 = 4.
  4. Subtract from 12 for your club number: 12 − 4 = 8, so use your 8-iron.
  5. Round UP for slight uphill, DOWN for slight downhill chips.
  6. Slow/High incline green: subtract from 11 or 10. Fast/High decline green: subtract from 13 or 14.
  7. Club Key: LW=12, SW=11, GW/PW=10, 9i=9, 8i=8, 7i=7, etc.

💥 Pre-Shot Routine — Wedge Shots Routine First ▼

Use this routine for all pitch shots — full wedges and partial swings from inside 100 yards.

  1. Know your exact carry number. Before the round, you should know your 3 swing-length distances for each wedge (8-to-4, 9-to-3, hands to ear). Use that number, not a guess. If you don't know it — take one extra club and swing easier.
  2. Choose your swing length, not your club. The question is never just "which club?" — it's "which club at which swing length?" Pick both before stepping up.
  3. Check wind and elevation. Add one club going uphill or into wind. Take one off downhill or downwind. Adjust carry distance accordingly.
  4. Aim at a safe landing zone, not the pin. Find the fat part of the green. The pin is a bonus. A center-green wedge that two-putts beats a pin-hunting wedge that spins into a bunker every time.
  5. Two practice swings — feel the length. Rehearse the exact swing length you selected. Feel where your hands stop in the backswing. Match that in the real swing.
  6. Set up with correct ball position. PW: center. GW/SW: slightly forward. LW: near left instep. Slight shaft lean. Weight slightly forward.
  7. One look at the target. Commit. Swing. Accelerate through — deceleration is the #1 cause of chunked wedges.
Clock cue: Hands to hip = 8 o'clock. Hands to shoulder = 9 o'clock. Hands to ear = 11 o'clock. Follow-through mirrors your backswing. If you stop early on the follow-through you decelerated — start the sequence over.

Wedge Shank Fix — 4-Phase StationTechnique▼

Phase 1 — Diagnosis (0-8 min): Identify shank type. Out-to-In (most common): steep, across, hosel leads, paired with a slice. In-to-Out (less common): path pushes right, ball squirts right.

Phase 2 — Barrier Drill (8-18 min): Place a range bucket or head cover just outside the toe at address. Start with half-speed swings. 10 slow practice swings → 10 half-speed chips → 10 full wedge swings. Cue: "Swing around the box, not through it."

Phase 3 — Weight and Balance (18-28 min): Option A: Toes on slightly elevated surface to force heel pressure. Option B: Load trail heel on backswing, transfer to lead heel at impact. Cue: "Sit into your heels like leaning back into a chair."

Phase 4 — Tee Gate (28-38 min): Push two tees into the ground — one inside the heel, one outside the toe — about 1/4 inch wider than the clubface. Threading clean = progress. 5 slow-motion swings → 10 at pace → 5 without gate. Cue: "Thread the needle — miss both tees."

⚙️ Pre-Shot Routine — Irons Routine First ▼

Use this routine for all iron shots — from a 9-iron approach to a long iron off a tight lie.

  1. Get your yardage. Pace it off or use your GPS app. Know the exact carry distance to the front of the green, the middle, and the pin. Hit to the middle unless the pin is accessible from the safe side.
  2. Account for conditions. Uphill = one club up. Downhill = one club down. Wind in face = one to two clubs up. Tailwind = one club down. Rough = one club up minimum.
  3. Pick your target — not the flag. Choose a spot on the green that is in the safe half relative to where the trouble is. Aim there. Commit to it.
  4. Stand behind the ball and build your alignment. Pick an intermediate target 2–3 feet in front of the ball on your target line. Step in, set the clubface to that intermediate target, then set your feet parallel to the line.
  5. Check your setup. Ball position center to slightly forward. Posture tall with slight hip hinge. Grip pressure light — about a 4 out of 10.
  6. One practice swing — same tempo as your real swing. Feel the weight transfer. Lower body initiates. Don't rush.
  7. One look at the target. Eyes back to the ball. One thought maximum — whatever your swing cue is for today.
  8. Swing with commitment. Trust the club. Trust the yardage. Trust the routine.
The golden rule: Never stand over a shot without a clear target in mind. "At the green" is not a target. A specific spot on the green is. The routine locks in your target before you swing.

⚙️ Pre-Shot Routine — Long Irons & Hybrids Routine First ▼

Use this routine for all long iron and hybrid shots — 4-iron through 5-wood off the deck or from a tight lie.

  1. Get your exact carry yardage. Long clubs have the largest distance variance of any club in the bag. Know the carry — not the total roll-out — to the front of the green, center, and pin. Use GPS or pacing.
  2. Account for elevation and conditions. Long irons and hybrids are more affected by uphill and downhill than shorter clubs. Add one full club uphill; subtract one downhill. Wind at your back means one less club; into wind means one more minimum.
  3. Pick a wide, safe target. Long irons are not precision tools — aim for the center of the green or the widest part of the fairway. Do not challenge trouble with a long iron.
  4. Stand behind the ball. Pick an intermediate target 2–3 feet ahead on your line. Walk in and set the clubface first, then feet.
  5. Widen your stance slightly. Long iron stance is slightly wider than short irons for stability. Check posture — tall with slight hip hinge, weight balanced.
  6. One practice swing at 75% tempo. Feel the shallow, sweeping motion. If the practice swing felt choppy or steep, reset — steep long iron swings are topped or chunked.
  7. One look. Trust the swing. Commit.
Key thought: Tempo is everything with long irons and hybrids. A smooth 80% swing makes solid contact. An aggressive 100% swing creates mishits. Commit to the swing you can repeat, not the swing that feels impressive.

🚀 Pre-Shot Routine — Driver Routine First ▼

Use this routine every time you stand on a tee box — driver or otherwise.

  1. Read the hole before you pull a club. Where is the OB? Where is the water? Where is the trouble on the approach if you miss in a certain direction? Know this before deciding on your club and target.
  2. Pick your landing zone, not a line. Identify a zone about 30 yards wide in the fairway — the zone you want the ball in. This is your target, not a thin line.
  3. Choose your club for the landing zone, not for maximum distance. If driver doesn't keep you in the zone, hit 3-wood. A shot in the fairway with a 3-wood is worth 40 yards of OB driver every time.
  4. Aim for your shot shape. If you draw, aim down the right side. If you fade, aim down the left. The ball will curve to your landing zone — work with your natural shape, not against it.
  5. Pick an intermediate target. Find something 3–5 feet ahead of the ball on your exact start line — a discoloration, a tee, a divot. Align to that.
  6. Take your practice swing. Smooth and full. Feel the tempo. Wide takeaway, full turn, balanced finish. If the practice swing felt rushed, reset and take another.
  7. One waggle, one look, commit. Waggle relaxes grip tension. One final look at the landing zone. Eyes back to the ball — specifically the inside back of the ball. That is your contact point.
  8. Trust the routine. Swing freely. The thinking is done. Swing with full commitment. A confident 85% swing beats a tentative 100% every time.
Tee box time limit: From the moment you step up to the ball, your routine should take no more than 25–30 seconds. A long routine is usually a sign of indecision — make your decisions before you step in, then execute.

If a player cannot attend the pre-meet practice session, direct them to this section — they can work through the skills independently at any course using the same framework.
How to Use This Section Each course has its own prep plan. Work through the hole-by-hole stations in order, spending approximately 15 minutes per hole. Focus on the specific shot types and distances listed — these are the exact situations you will face on meet day. Practice with intention, not just repetition.

Evansville Golf Club — Course Prep Practice Plan
18-hole meet · Par 72 · ~15 minutes per hole · Practice range clubs first
Tee Shots Large trees and tight doglegs define this course. Position beats power — a 200-yard club in the fairway beats a driver in the trees every time. Know your shot shape. The road crossing on hole 2 is out of bounds.
Approach Shots / Shots into Greens Greens are bent grass and consistently fast. Always leave yourself BELOW the hole — putting from above is very difficult. Short-sided chip shots are very difficult from the right side. Aim center of the green on every approach.
Greens / Putting Fast bent grass with significant undulation. Practice lag putts from above the hole to feel the speed before your round. Never be above the hole if you can avoid it.

Hole 2 / 5
Long Par 4 + Below-Hole Putting
Simulate Evansville Hole 2 (510 yds, Road Hole, OB road crossing). Hit 2 controlled iron or wood shots off the tee. Pick up. Drop at 160 yards and hit a full iron to the center of the green. Putt from both below and above the hole — practice the speed difference.
Hole 3 / 8
Danger Hole — Punch-Outs + 100-Yard Wedge
Simulate Evansville Hole 3 (Danger Hole, trees pinch at 200–250 yds). Hit 1 cautious iron tee shot landing short of the narrow zone. Pick up. Drop at 100 yards and hit a controlled wedge to the center of the green. Practice 3 chip-out punch shots from rough back to the fairway — this hole demands a 3-shot par 4 mentality.
Hole 4 / 7
Par 3 — Mid-Iron Center + Recovery
Simulate Evansville Hole 4 (198 yds). Hit 2 mid-iron shots to the center of the green — do not aim at pins near the edges. Practice chip recovery from just short of the green.
Hole 6 / 9
Best Par Opportunity — Control + 2-Putt Routine
Simulate Evansville Hole 6 (340 yds, best par on the front). Hit 1 hybrid or 2-wood for control, leaving 100–150 yards in. Drop at 120 yards and hit a full iron to the center. Practice a 2-putt par routine from 20 feet — this is the hole you must convert.
Hole 12 / 14
OB Discipline + Lag Putts
Simulate Evansville Hole 5 (390 yds, OB left, water right). Hit 2 tee shots staying center — nothing left. Drop at 140 yards and hit to the green. Practice lag putts from 30+ feet — undulating greens will leave you with these often.
Hole 13 / 17
Par 3 Fast-Green Speed Control
Simulate Evansville par 3s. Hit 2 shots from 140–160 yards to the center of the green. Practice 3-putt avoidance from above the hole — putt these aggressively and leave yourself below.
Hole 15 / 11
Par 5 — 3-Shot Discipline + 40-Foot Lags
Simulate Evansville par 5s. Play 3 controlled shots: iron off tee, iron to 80–100 yards, wedge to center of green. DO NOT go for the green in two. Practice lag putts from 40 feet.
Hole 16 / 18
Par Opportunity — Driver + Wedge + Steady Putting
Simulate Evansville Hole 16 (Par Opportunity, 500 yds). Hit 1 driver if confident and straight. Drop at 100 yards and practice a wedge to the center. Practice holding your finish and reading break — greens reward patient putting.

New Glarus "Green to Tee" Developmental Golf Program
Sequential skill development from the green backward to the tee
Program Purpose Players learn through a Green-to-Tee approach: putts first, then chips, pitch shots, rescue shots, and full swings — each skill taught sequentially once a player has graduated to the next division. Players in different divisions can play together in the same group using Ready Golf. No player should cause significant delays to the group.
Stroke Limit No more than 7 shots on any single hole. If a player reaches 7, they pick up and continue practicing the remaining holes — but must wait until the next round to graduate to the next division.
Playing Together — How It Works Players A, B, C, D are in a group. Player A (White Tee Division) tees off from the white tees while the other 3 wait. After A hits, all 4 walk to Player B's division distance and wait for B to hit. Continue to the nearest ball or next player's division distance, whichever comes first. A player in the Green Division would wait until all players have hit onto the green, then drop their ball on the green and play in. With Ready Golf, this takes no longer than a group all playing from the same tees.
Green Division — Putting Only
Graduate: Shoot 36 for 9 holes
▼

Skills taught: About a putting green · How to read slope and break · How to mark and line a putt · How to hold a putter · Putting stance · How to hit a putt.

Etiquette: Who should putt first · Avoiding walking on others' lines · How to fix a ball mark on the green.

Playing format: Place a ball on the green at each hole. Player has 4 shots to putt the ball into the hole. Graduate by shooting 36 for 9 holes.

Chipping Division — Off the Green
Graduate: Shoot 36 for 9 holes
▼

Skills taught: How to hold a chipping club · Reinforce green reading · How to line up the clubface along the intended line · How to chip a golf ball using different clubs (The Rule of 12).

Playing format: Place a ball off the green at each hole. Player has 4 shots to hole out. Putting skills are also reinforced each session.

▼

150 Yard Division — Sand Shots Added
Place ball 150 yds from center of green

200 Yard Division — Punch Shots Added
Place ball 200 yds from center of green
▼

Skills taught: Full swing from 200 yards to get close to the middle of the green. How to hit punch shots to advance the ball when in trouble. Sand shots, rough shots, pitching, chipping and putting reinforced.

Yellow Tee Division — Analytics Begin
▼

New focus: Players begin tracking Fairways in Regulation (FIR), Greens in Regulation (GIR), and number of putts per round. These analytics identify areas requiring the most improvement to graduate to the next tee distance.

Red Tee Division — Power Development
▼

Focus: Developing flexibility and power for the golf swing to shorten the course off the tee. Players continue using analytics to identify and work on specific areas of improvement during practice sessions.

White Tee Division — Short Game Scoring Focus
▼

Focus: Continued flexibility and power development, plus improving scoring inside 100 yards. Analytics continue to guide practice priorities.

Blue Tee Division — Advanced Scoring Focus
▼

Focus: Continued flexibility and power development, plus improving scoring inside 150 yards. Analytics continue to drive practice priorities and identify weaknesses to address each session.

Strength Training for Game Improvement
2× per week · 30 min per session · Golf-specific strength, power, flexibility & injury prevention
Program Design Two workouts per week — one at home with no equipment, one using school equipment. Both target the same five pillars: rotational power, hip strength, core stability, upper body connection, and mobility/injury prevention. Do each workout once per week throughout the season. The home workout requires only a floor and your bodyweight.
Coach's Note Flexibility and strength are not optional — they are the difference between a 200-yard drive and a 230-yard drive, and between a healthy season and a missed one. Commit to both days every week. The 30 minutes pays back in every swing you take.
